DreamHost Homepage Redesign

What Hostinger, BlueHost, SiteGround, and GoDaddy put on their homepage

And what it means for DreamHost's redesign.

Executive Summary
All four competitors have converged on the same playbook — price or AI — and their homepage strategies reveal clear patterns in how each approaches conversion.
  • Pricing is a race to the bottom. Hostinger advertises $1.99/mo and BlueHost starts at $3.99/mo. GoDaddy leads with $4.99/mo for domain + Airo AI. Hostinger requires a 48-month commitment that renews at 5.5x the intro rate; BlueHost requires 36 months. SiteGround shows no pricing at all.
  • AI is everywhere but owned by no one. GoDaddy has gone all-in on "Airo" as a branded AI product line. Hostinger has 13+ AI products, SiteGround aggregates ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini into AI Studio, BlueHost badges everything "AI built in." No competitor has made AI a defensible differentiator — though GoDaddy is betting the hardest with Airo branding across every section.
  • Trust strategies diverge sharply. Hostinger leads with press logos and volume stats (4M clients). BlueHost publishes speed benchmarks (247ms) and stacks WordPress.org credibility. SiteGround puts Trustpilot 4.9 directly in the hero. GoDaddy plays the scale card — "millions of customers" and "20+ million trust us" — leaning on sheer volume over quality signals.
  • Ownership goes unmentioned. BlueHost is PE-owned (Newfold Digital). Hostinger is VC-backed. SiteGround was acquired. GoDaddy is publicly traded (NYSE: GDDY). None of them talk about it — but the ownership structures shape every pricing, support, and product decision on these pages.

Competitor Portraits

Four homepages, four strategies

Hostinger
Price-first, AI-everywhere
"Launch your website today with 85% off"

Hostinger's homepage is a conversion machine built around a single number: $1.99. The hero doesn't mention AI, WordPress, or any specific product — it sells a price point and creates urgency with a countdown timer. Product education comes later. AI products (Horizons, Kodee, AI Website Builder) are positioned mid-page under the balanced headline "Human-led, AI-powered" — acknowledging AI skepticism while still claiming capability. The page is dense: 12 sections covering everything from email marketing to a customer website gallery. Pricing requires a 48-month commitment and renews at $10.99-$25.99/mo (a 5.5x increase over introductory pricing).

BlueHost
WordPress identity, trust stacking
"Grow Online with Confidence"

BlueHost's homepage stacks trust signals like a closing argument. The hero leads with credibility: WordPress.org recommendation, 30-day guarantee, "5 million WordPress users." There's a phone number in the top bar — unique among the four — signaling a consultation-first sales model. Every product, feature, and CTA ties back to WordPress. The page's most distinctive asset is a speed benchmark section showing BlueHost's 247ms US load time compared to competitors in a bar chart. Pricing is buried in section 8 of 10, after extensive trust-building. The page ends with a FAQ section (strong for SEO) and a "Request a consultation" lead gen form.

SiteGround
Premium platform, no price on the page
"Build, host, grow your online presence"

SiteGround's homepage is the leanest and most confident of the four. No pricing, no urgency, no discounts. The hero puts Trustpilot's 4.9/5 stars directly in the hero — letting the rating do the selling. Below, six product cards are shown with equal visual weight (WordPress, Website Builder, Online Store, Web Hosting, Email Marketing, Coderick AI) — a deliberate "platform" statement. The most distinctive move: AI Studio, included in all plans, which aggregates ChatGPT, Claude, Google, and Gemini into one dashboard. Instead of building proprietary AI, SiteGround gives you access to everyone else's. The support section — "Real support, real humans 24/7" with 98% satisfaction for 10+ years — is the sharpest counter-positioning against the AI-everything trend.

GoDaddy
Domain-first, AI brand play with Airo
"Get started for just $4.99"

The "Everyone works with GoDaddy" section plays the scale card — multiple audience types, "20+ million trust us for domains." The page closes with another video case study, a "We love to help. Conclusion

What This Means for DreamHost

Every competitor analyzed here has converged on the same two levers: aggressive introductory pricing and AI feature branding. Hostinger and BlueHost compete on price floors ($1.99/mo and $3.99/mo respectively, both requiring multi-year commitments). GoDaddy and Hostinger compete on AI feature volume. SiteGround opts out of both races entirely, selling confidence and platform breadth instead. None of them are telling a story that DreamHost can't tell better.

Short-Term Opportunities

  • Lead with pricing clarity. Hostinger does disclose renewal rates on their homepage ("Renews at $10.99/mo"), but it's buried beneath the promotional pricing and 48-month commitment. BlueHost's $3.99/mo requires 36 months with renewal details pushed to fine print. DreamHost can go further — not just disclosing renewal rates, but making honest pricing the headline message. Instead of leading with a teaser rate, lead with the real cost of ownership.
  • Own the trust position. SiteGround proved that putting Trustpilot in the hero works — their 4.9 rating does the selling for them. DreamHost's own ratings and 29+ years of operation are underutilized. Trustpilot ratings, customer testimonials, and longevity stats belong above the fold, not buried in a footer.
  • Make AI tangible, not aspirational. GoDaddy has 20+ mentions of "Airo" but the homepage never shows it working. Hostinger lists 13 AI products but they blend into the background. The opportunity: show the actual product in action — a live demo, a before/after, a real site built with Remixer — instead of just badging features with "AI."

Medium & Long-Term Differentiation

  • Independence as a feature. BlueHost is owned by Newfold Digital (private equity). Hostinger is VC-backed. SiteGround was acquired. GoDaddy answers to Wall Street. None of them mention it — but ownership shapes every decision, from support staffing to pricing strategy to product roadmaps. DreamHost's independence isn't just a brand value; it's a structural advantage. An independent company can prioritize customer experience over quarterly earnings, invest in support without a PE firm demanding margin expansion, and keep pricing honest because there's no pressure to extract maximum revenue per user.
  • Support as a competitive moat. SiteGround's "Real support, real humans 24/7" with a 98% satisfaction rate for 10+ years is the strongest counter-positioning on any of these pages. DreamHost has the same story to tell — and with in-house expertise spanning 29+ years, the credibility to back it up. As competitors increasingly route support through AI chatbots (Hostinger's Kodee, GoDaddy's automated flows), human expertise becomes rarer and more valuable.
  • Performance with proof. BlueHost is the only competitor publishing speed benchmarks (247ms server response). That's because they can. DreamHost's infrastructure — NVMe storage, global data centers, 100% uptime guarantee — deserves the same treatment. Publish real benchmarks. Let the numbers speak.
  • Platform depth over feature sprawl. GoDaddy's homepage is a wall of products and features — hosting, domains, Airo AI, email, websites, marketing tools — with no clear hierarchy. Hostinger stacks 13 AI products. The risk of "more features" is "more confusion." DreamHost can differentiate by doing fewer things exceptionally well and making the path from signup to live site unmistakably clear.

The Biggest Gap: Nobody Owns Small Business

The most striking finding across all four competitors: none of them explicitly target small businesses on their homepage. Every competitor sells "build a website" or "get online" — generic language aimed at anyone with a credit card. The word "small business" doesn't appear in a single hero headline.

  • Hostinger targets price-sensitive beginners and side-hustlers. "Your website," "your project," "your vision" — individual language, not business language.
  • BlueHost leans business-adjacent with "Grow Online with Confidence" and a consultation offer, but never claims the small business audience explicitly.
  • GoDaddy comes closest with "What's hot for your business?" but frames itself as universal ("Everyone works with GoDaddy"), diluting the positioning.
  • SiteGround is entirely product-focused — six product cards, pick one. No audience segmentation at all.

More critically, no competitor bundles small-business-specific tools as a homepage selling point. No local SEO. No review management. No business planning. No legal compliance. They all sell the technical deliverable (hosting, domains, websites) rather than the business outcome (get found locally, earn more reviews, look professional, stay compliant).

DreamHost already has the pieces to own this position: Remixer for AI website building, Pro Services for hands-on help, expert human support with 29+ years of experience, AI Business Tools (Planner, Advisor, Content Creator), and an entire suite of free marketing tools spanning local business, legal compliance, email marketing, and SEO. That's not a hosting product — that's a small business growth platform.

Two approaches to consider for the homepage:

  • Verticalized + social proof combined. Lead with "Built for Small Business" positioning, with real customer success stories woven directly into the narrative. Social proof validates the vertical claim — every testimonial reinforces that this platform was made for businesses like yours.
  • Vertical positioning + trust as separate sections. A clear small-business section (tools, features, business outcomes) paired with a distinct trust section (Trustpilot, longevity, testimonials, uptime). Gives each room to breathe and lets the trust section stand on its own for visitors who don't self-identify as "small business."

The Positioning Gap

There is a clear gap in the market for a hosting provider that combines honest pricing, proven performance, genuine human support, and real AI tools that demonstrably work — all explicitly built for small businesses. No competitor currently occupies this position.

SiteGround comes closest on trust but avoids pricing and audience entirely. BlueHost comes closest on WordPress credibility but undermines it with aggressive intro pricing. Hostinger leads on price but targets individuals, not businesses. GoDaddy leads on AI branding but spreads itself too thin to be convincing on any single dimension.

DreamHost doesn't need to out-discount Hostinger or out-feature GoDaddy. The homepage should do what no competitor's currently does: speak directly to small business owners, show real tools solving real business problems, price honestly, and make the case that an independent company — accountable to customers, not investors — builds better hosting for the businesses that need it most.
